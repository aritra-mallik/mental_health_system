https://www.psychiatry.org/patients-families/seasonal-affective-disorder
Seasonal affective disorder (SAD) is a type of depression that follows a recurring seasonal pattern, most commonly beginning in fall or winter and improving in spring or summer. It involves symptoms similar to major depression, such as low mood, loss of interest, fatigue, and changes in sleep or appetite. The condition is linked to reduced sunlight, which can disrupt the body’s internal clock and affect brain chemicals like serotonin and melatonin. Certain factors, including gender, age, and location, may increase the risk of developing SAD. Treatment options include light therapy, psychotherapy (especially cognitive behavioral therapy), medication, and lifestyle changes to help manage symptoms effectively.

Persistent depressive disorder (dysthymia) is a long-lasting form of depression that may be less intense than major depression but continues for years.
Its key feature is a chronic low or sad mood, along with symptoms like low energy, poor concentration, sleep problems, and low self-esteem.
Unlike major depression, the duration of symptoms—typically two years or more—is more important than their severity for diagnosis.
The condition often begins early in life and may feel like a normal part of a person’s personality, which can prevent them from seeking help.
Treatment usually involves a combination of psychotherapy and medication, which can reduce symptoms and improve quality of life over time.

Attachment styles are patterns of how people form emotional bonds and relationships, shaped mainly by early experiences with caregivers.
There are four main types: secure, anxious (preoccupied), avoidant (dismissive), and disorganized (fearful), each reflecting different levels of trust, intimacy, and emotional regulation.
Secure attachment is the healthiest, where individuals feel comfortable with closeness and independence and can maintain stable relationships.
Insecure styles include anxious (fear of abandonment and need for reassurance), avoidant (emotional distance and independence), and disorganized (a mix of fear and desire for closeness).
These attachment patterns can influence adult relationships, but they are not fixed and can change over time with awareness and effort.
